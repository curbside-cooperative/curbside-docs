# Pallet Wood Safety Statement

Curbside Community Farms Cooperative uses reclaimed pallet wood to build raised garden beds for boulevard food gardens in Victoria, BC. This statement describes what we know about the safety of that wood, what we do to manage the risks, and where uncertainties remain. It covers chemical and contamination risks only -- physical hazards (nails, splinters) are addressed separately.

We have done our best to ground this document in reliable sources and invite anyone to send us information we may have missed, or to simply share concerns, either through our [contact page](https://www.curbside.coop/contact/) or by emailing us directly at [hello@curbside.coop](mailto:hello@curbside.coop).

> [!NOTE]- Quick summary
> We source pallets locally from benign-cargo suppliers, reject any marked MB (methyl bromide) or showing signs of contamination, and **plane all soil-contact surfaces** to mechanically remove the outer layer where surface treatments reside. See [[#What you can confidently say]] for the full list of claims we stand behind, and [[#What you should acknowledge]] for the uncertainties we accept.

---

## How we source and prepare pallet wood

### Where our pallets come from

Our pallets come from local suppliers: furniture retailers, plumbing and irrigation wholesalers, and a local hardware store. These businesses receive pallets as packaging for their own incoming goods and would otherwise discard them. They do not deal primarily in toxic or hazardous substances -- furniture suppliers in particular handle benign cargo.

That said, we cannot trace the full history of any individual pallet. Our suppliers receive pallets in mixed piles, not sorted by origin. Many pallets have circulated through multiple use cycles before arriving at the supplier. The supplier often does not know what a given pallet carried before it reached them.

### Our acceptance criteria

We accept a pallet only if it passes all of the following checks:

- **Unmarked pallets** are accepted. Under the Canada-US bilateral exemption to ISPM 15, domestic pallets are not required to carry any stamp or marking (see Key Facts below). An unstamped pallet in a Canadian supplier's pile is, in the vast majority of cases, a domestic Canadian or US pallet that was never required to be stamped.

- **ISPM 15-marked pallets** are accepted only if the stamp shows both **HT** (heat treated) and a country code of **CA** (Canada) or **US** (United States). This confirms the pallet was heat-treated -- a purely thermal process with no chemical inputs -- and produced under North American regulatory oversight.

- **MB-marked pallets** are rejected without exception. MB indicates methyl bromide fumigation, a toxic ozone-depleting pesticide. In practice, after collecting over 100 pallets, we have never encountered one.

- **Pallets that are excessively dirty, stained, discolored, or have unusual odors** (chemical, musty, solvent-like) are rejected. These may indicate cargo spills or chemical contamination.

- **Painted or colored pallets** (blue, red, brown) are rejected. These typically belong to commercial pooling systems (CHEP, PECO, iGPS) with unknown chemical maintenance histories.

### How we prepare the wood

Every board that will be directly exposed to soil is **planed** before assembly. A standard planer pass removes approximately 1.6 mm (1/16 inch) of wood from each face [A]. This is the single most important preparation step we take, and its significance is explained in the Key Facts section below.

No liners, oils, sealants, or other coatings are applied to the wood. The growing medium sits directly against freshly planed wood surfaces.

## Key facts

This section presents the core factual claims on which our safety reasoning depends. Each claim is supported by specific sources. If any of these facts are wrong, our safety assessment would need to change -- which is why we present them explicitly and invite scrutiny.

### 1. Canada does not permit methyl bromide fumigation for domestic pallet production

The CFIA's Heat Treatment Program (Directive D-13-01) certifies only heat treatment for Canadian-produced ISPM 15 wood packaging material. Methyl bromide fumigation is explicitly excluded [1][2]. Since January 1, 2005, the manufacture, import, and export of methyl bromide has been prohibited in Canada except for quarantine/pre-shipment (QPS) applications and critical use exemptions under the Montreal Protocol [3][4]. In June 2025, Health Canada's PMRA completed a re-evaluation maintaining these restrictions [5][6]. Any HT-stamped pallet bearing a CA country code was heat-treated, not fumigated.

In the United States, MB is still technically permitted for ISPM 15 compliance under the QPS exemption, and the ALSC certifies both HT and MB programs. However, in practice, MB treatment of pallets is extremely rare -- heat treatment dominates, and no certified MB fumigators were reported as active under the official IPPC program according to industry reporting [7].

**What this means for us:** The risk of encountering an MB-treated pallet from a North American supplier is very low, and our policy of rejecting any pallet marked MB eliminates the remaining risk from marked pallets. The only residual scenario would be a fraudulently stamped pallet (see section on stamp fraud below).

### 2. The Canada-US bilateral exemption means unstamped domestic pallets are normal, not suspicious

Since 2005, wood packaging materials moving between Canada and the United States have been exempt from both the ISPM 15 treatment requirement and the marking requirement, provided the wood originates from either country [8][9][10]. A phase-out of this exemption was announced in 2012 but has never been implemented as of the most recent available information [7].

This means that an unmarked pallet circulating in Canada is, in all likelihood, a domestic Canadian pallet or a US pallet that crossed the border under the exemption. It has no stamp because it was never required to have one. An unmarked pallet is not a red flag -- it is the normal state of a domestic pallet.

**What this means for us:** Accepting unmarked pallets is not a relaxation of our standards. It reflects the regulatory reality that most domestic pallets carry no markings at all.

### 3. Anti-sapstain fungicide treatments penetrate less than 1-2 mm into the wood surface

Conventional anti-sapstain fungicides applied by dip or spray treatment -- the standard method used on pallets and fresh lumber -- penetrate less than 1-2 mm from the surface [11]. This is because dip and spray treatments rely on superficial capillary action during brief contact (seconds to minutes of submersion). The fungicide sits in the outermost layer of the wood.

Some newer "mobile" or "diffusable" fungicides can penetrate deeper (10-20 mm), but these are less common and designed for specific applications [11].

### 4. A standard planer pass removes approximately 1.6 mm (1/16 inch) of wood per face

Standard wood planers remove between 0.8 mm (1/32 inch) and 1.6 mm (1/16 inch) per pass [A]. Even a single light pass at 0.8 mm removes the majority of a 1-2 mm fungicide layer. A standard pass at 1.6 mm removes essentially all of it.

**What these two facts mean together:** Planing the soil-contact faces of our pallet wood removes the layer where surface-applied fungicides reside. This is a mechanical removal of the treated wood itself -- not a chemical neutralization, not a coating, not a barrier. The wood that touches the soil is freshly exposed interior wood that was never in contact with any surface treatment.

### 5. Multiple fungicides are used on North American pallets -- not just PQ-8, and not on all pallets

The claim sometimes made that "North American manufacturers use FDA-approved PQ-8" is an oversimplification. The actual landscape is more complex [12][13][14]:

- **PQ-8 / PQ-80 (copper 8-quinolinolate)** is the only anti-sapstain fungicide with FDA/EPA approval for incidental food contact under 21 CFR 178.3800 [15]. It is manufactured by ISK Biocides. PalletOne, a major manufacturer, applies it only when customers specifically request mold inhibitor -- it is not standard on all their pallets [14].

- **NeXgen (chlorothalonil + methylene bis thiocyanate/MBT)** is not food-contact approved. Chlorothalonil is under EPA mitigation measures (not banned, but restricted for aquatic/soil concerns) and is banned in the EU [12].

- **IPBC (3-iodo-2-propynyl butylcarbamate)** is EPA-registered for wood coatings and registered in both the US and Canada [12].

- **Propiconazole** is a triazole fungicide, EPA-registered for above-ground wood uses. Canada's PMRA continued its registration after re-evaluation [16].

- **TBP (tribromophenol)** is banned in both the US and Canada but is still used in some South American lumber that enters the North American supply chain. It was the chemical implicated in the 2009-2010 Tylenol/TBA contamination incident [12][17].

**Many pallets receive no fungicide treatment at all.** Treatment is demand-driven, not universal. "Many pallet users do not allow any chemical treatments" (Conner Industries, per Pallet Enterprise) [12]. Food and pharmaceutical customers increasingly require chemical-free (heat treatment only) pallets.

There is no published industry-wide data on what percentage of pallets are fungicide-treated.

### 6. "FDA-approved for incidental food contact" does not cover prolonged soil contact in garden beds

PQ-8's regulatory basis is 21 CFR 178.3800 -- "Preservatives for wood" [15]. This regulation permits preservatives on "wooden articles that are used or intended for use in packaging, transporting, or holding raw agricultural products." The intended use is brief, indirect contact: the pallet touches the box that holds the food, or a crate holds produce for transport.

A raised garden bed with soil pressed against the wood continuously for months is a categorically different exposure scenario. The FDA does not regulate pallets directly and "doesn't have the authority to put their stamp of approval on them" (PalletOne) [14]. Citing FDA food-contact approval as evidence of safety for garden bed use would be an overstatement of what the regulation actually covers.

### 7. PQ-8 has very low water solubility but is not rated for ground contact

PQ-8 (copper 8-quinolinolate) has a water solubility of 0.07 mg/L at 25 degrees C -- it is practically insoluble [18][19]. This extremely low solubility suggests it would leach very slowly into surrounding soil. The copper in PQ-8 is chelated (bound to 8-hydroxyquinoline), unlike the more readily leachable copper in CCA-treated wood.

However, PQ-8 is explicitly "not effective for ground contact use" and is "intended for above ground use only" according to University of Florida IFAS [20]. The EPA flagged leachability as a data gap in its Reregistration Eligibility Decision [21]. No specific studies were found on PQ-8 leaching into garden soil from pallet wood.

Related research on copper-based wood preservatives (copper azole, not PQ-8 specifically) from Oregon State University found that after one year in raised beds, there was "no difference in copper content in vegetable matter" between treated and untreated boxes, and leached copper "quickly bound up in clay and organic matter" [22]. However, copper azole and PQ-8 are different compounds with different leaching profiles, so this finding cannot be directly transferred.

**What this means for us:** The low water solubility of PQ-8 is genuinely reassuring, but it would be dishonest to cite the FDA food-contact approval as proof of safety for our specific use. Our primary mitigation is not chemical properties -- it is the mechanical removal of the treated surface layer by planing.

### 8. Fungicides can be applied at the sawmill, not just the pallet manufacturer

Fungicide treatment can happen at two points in the supply chain [12][13]:

1. **At the sawmill or lumber mill** -- prophylactic treatment by dipping or spraying freshly sawn lumber at high moisture content (>40%). This is more efficient because large volumes of lumber can be treated before being cut into pallet components.

2. **At the pallet manufacturer** -- dip tanks or spray application on components or finished pallets.

This means that even if a pallet manufacturer says they do not apply fungicide, their lumber supplier may have already treated the wood. This is not a gap in our protocol -- planing addresses surface treatments regardless of when they were applied.

## Detailed risk analysis

### Methyl bromide fumigation -- Risk level: LOW

**What it is:** Methyl bromide (MB) is a broad-spectrum fumigant classified as an ozone-depleting substance. It is acutely toxic and can cause respiratory damage, neurological symptoms, and skin irritation. The U.S. EPA classifies it as "highly acute toxic" [23].

**Why the risk is low for us:**

- Canada banned manufacture, import, and export of MB in 2005, with only narrow QPS exemptions remaining [3][4]. The CFIA's HT program does not permit MB for domestic pallet production [1][2].

- Any legitimately MB-treated pallet would carry an MB stamp, which our protocol rejects on sight.

- The US-Canada bilateral exemption means the unmarked pallets in our supply chain are domestic pallets, not international shipments that might have been fumigated abroad.

- In practice, MB-treated pallets have become exceedingly rare even in the US. Heat treatment has dominated for over a decade [7].

**Residual risk:** The only pathway for MB-treated wood to enter our supply is through a fraudulently stamped pallet (see stamp fraud section) or an extremely old pallet predating current standards. Both are implausible in practice.

### Fungicide and anti-stain treatments -- Risk level: LOW

**What it is:** Some pallet producers and lumber mills apply anti-sapstain fungicides to prevent mold growth on fresh wood. These are surface treatments applied by dip or spray, separate from and in addition to heat treatment [12][13].

**Why the risk is low for us:**

- **Planing removes the treated layer.** Surface-applied fungicides penetrate less than 1-2 mm [11]. Our planing step removes approximately 1.6 mm per face, mechanically stripping the fungicide-containing wood before the board is used in a garden bed.

- **Not all pallets are treated.** Fungicide application is demand-driven, not universal. Many manufacturers do not apply any chemical treatments [12].

- **North American fungicides are regulated.** The chemicals used on pallets in Canada and the US are EPA/PMRA-registered products. TBP, the most concerning fungicide historically used on pallets, is banned in both countries [12].

- **Even when present, amounts are small.** When fungicides are applied to pallets, concentrations on the wood surface are typically 5-10 ppm [12].

**Residual risk:** We cannot verify whether any given pallet was fungicide-treated, and we cannot verify whether the lumber supplier treated the wood before the pallet manufacturer received it. However, because planing removes the layer where these treatments reside, the question of whether fungicide was applied becomes largely moot for the soil-contact surfaces.

Non-planed surfaces (the outer faces of bed walls, which do not contact soil or growing medium) may retain surface treatments. These surfaces are exposed to air and weather, not soil, and do not present a pathway to food crops.

### Cargo contamination (spills and residues) -- Risk level: LOW-MODERATE

**What it is:** Pallets transport an enormous variety of goods. A spill during shipping -- chemicals, solvents, oils, paints, pesticides -- can leave residues embedded in the wood that are difficult or impossible to detect visually [24].

**Why this risk is moderated for us:**

- **Supplier vetting.** Our pallets come from furniture suppliers, plumbing/irrigation wholesalers, and a local hardware store. Furniture suppliers in particular deal in benign cargo. None of these suppliers specialize in hazardous materials, though a hardware store may occasionally receive some.

- **Visual and olfactory inspection.** We reject pallets that are excessively dirty, stained, discolored, or smelly. The Tylenol/TBA contamination incident demonstrated that chemical taints in wood are often detectable by odor -- that case involved a musty smell that consumers noticed in sealed medicine bottles [17][24].

- **Planing removes surface residues.** Any contaminant deposited on the surface of the wood by a cargo spill is removed along with the planed layer, to the same depth as fungicide treatments.

- **Colored/pooling pallets are rejected.** Commercial pooling pallets (CHEP, PECO, etc.) circulate through many industries with unknown cargo histories. By rejecting painted or colored pallets, we exclude the highest-uncertainty portion of the pallet supply.

> [!WARNING]- Diesel is the highest-risk common contaminant
> Diesel is thin enough to penetrate wood, non-volatile enough to remain rather than evaporate, and contains polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and other persistent compounds [35]. However, diesel contamination typically leaves visible staining and detectable odor. For pallets sourced from furniture retailers and plumbing wholesalers, prolonged pooling of diesel on pallet surfaces is uncommon.

**Residual risk:** This remains the least controllable risk factor. We cannot trace the cargo history of any individual pallet. Our layered mitigations (supplier vetting, inspection, planing) reduce the risk meaningfully, but they cannot eliminate it entirely. A colorless, odorless liquid spill on a pallet that subsequently dries and leaves no visible trace would not be detected by our inspection protocol.

### Stamp fraud / counterfeit HT marks -- Risk level: LOW

**What it is:** There have been documented criminal cases in the US of companies falsifying ISPM 15 heat-treatment stamps on untreated pallets. In one notable case, American Pallet Recycling LLC of New York was fined $100,000 after its owner pleaded guilty to stamping untreated pallets as ISPM 15 certified over a four-year period (2007-2011) [25][26].

**Why this risk is low for us:**

- Our pallets come from consistent local suppliers, not pallet recyclers or brokers who aggregate pallets from many sources.

- Stamp fraud is primarily an issue for international trade compliance, not for garden safety. A fraudulently HT-stamped pallet that was never actually heat-treated would typically just be an untreated domestic pallet -- the same wood, minus the heat step. The heat treatment itself is a pest control measure (killing insects), not a chemical decontamination step. A pallet that skipped heat treatment is not more chemically dangerous than one that received it.

- We also accept unmarked pallets under the CA/US bilateral exemption, so our safety case does not depend entirely on the HT stamp being authentic.

**Residual risk:** A more concerning fraud scenario would be a pallet falsely stamped HT that was actually MB-fumigated. This is highly implausible -- MB fumigation is a more expensive process than heat treatment, and the incentive for fraud runs in the opposite direction.

### Chromated copper arsenate (CCA) pressure treatment -- Risk level: VERY LOW

**What it is:** CCA is a wood preservative containing chromium, copper, and arsenic, historically used for outdoor structural lumber (decks, fences, playsets). It is classified as carcinogenic [27][28].

**Why this risk is essentially zero for us:** CCA pressure treatment was designed for structural lumber intended for long-term outdoor exposure, not for disposable shipping pallets. The process is expensive and serves a completely different purpose. There is no meaningful history of CCA being used on standard shipping pallets. CCA was also voluntarily phased out for residential uses in North America by the end of 2003 [27][28].

## What you can confidently say

Based on the available evidence, Curbside can state:

1. **We plane all soil-contact surfaces, mechanically removing the outer layer of wood where surface treatments reside.** Surface-applied fungicides penetrate less than 1-2 mm [11]; our planer removes approximately 1.6 mm per pass. This is our single strongest mitigation and it is based on straightforward physics, not assumptions about chemical safety.

2. **Methyl bromide fumigation is not used for domestic pallet production in Canada** and has been mostly banned since 2005 [1][2][3]. We reject any pallet marked MB. After collecting over 100 pallets, we have never encountered one.

3. **Unstamped pallets circulating in Canada are normal domestic pallets,** not imports of unknown origin. The Canada-US bilateral exemption to ISPM 15 means domestic pallets are not required to carry stamps or undergo treatment [8][9][10].

4. **Our suppliers deal primarily in benign goods** (furniture, plumbing supplies, hardware), not hazardous chemicals. We inspect every pallet and reject those showing signs of contamination.

5. **The fungicides used on North American pallets are regulated by the EPA and Health Canada's PMRA.** The most concerning historical pallet fungicide (TBP/tribromophenol) is banned in both Canada and the US [12].

6. **We do not apply any liners, oils, coatings, or other chemicals to the wood.** Growing medium sits against freshly planed, untreated wood.

## What you should acknowledge

Honesty requires acknowledging:

1. **We cannot trace the cargo history of any individual pallet.** Our suppliers receive pallets in mixed piles and do not track what each pallet carried. Visual and olfactory inspection catches obvious contamination but would miss colorless, odorless residues.

2. **We do not know whether any given pallet was fungicide-treated, or with which product.** While planing removes surface treatments, we cannot verify the presence or absence of treatment on specific boards.

3. **"FDA-approved for incidental food contact" does not mean "safe for prolonged soil contact in garden beds."** The FDA food-contact approval for PQ-8 covers pallets transporting food, not garden beds. We do not rely on this approval for our safety case -- we rely on planing to remove the treated layer.

4. **Planing addresses surface-applied treatments effectively, but its effectiveness against deeper cargo spills has limits.** Planing 1.6 mm removes fungicide dip treatments and brief surface contamination, but low-viscosity liquids (diesel, solvents) that pooled for hours to days can penetrate an estimated 2-5 mm on face grain -- beyond what planing removes.

5. **New, untreated lumber would eliminate all wood-related chemical uncertainty.** We use reclaimed pallets because they are free, abundant, and divert waste from landfill. This is a sustainability and cost decision.

6. **We have not conducted laboratory testing of our pallet wood or growing media for chemical contaminants.** Such testing would strengthen our evidence base. We rely instead on process controls (sourcing, inspection, planing) and the body of publicly available research cited in this document.
